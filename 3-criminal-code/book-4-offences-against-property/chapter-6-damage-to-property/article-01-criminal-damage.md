# Article 1 â€“ Criminal Damage

1. Criminal damage is the intentional or reckless destruction, damage, defacement, or impairment of property belonging to another, without lawful justification or excuse.

2. Criminal damage includes:
    (a) destroying or damaging tangible property, including buildings, vehicles, equipment, or personal possessions;
    (b) defacing property by graffiti, vandalism, or other means;
    (c) damaging or destroying digital property, data, or systems (which may also constitute cybercrime under Chapter 8 of this Book);
    (d) damaging crops, trees, animals, or agricultural property;
    (e) damaging property of cultural, historical, or public importance; or
    (f) any other act causing destruction or impairment of property.

3. Criminal damage is punished under Penalty Band C.

4. The penalty is aggravated to Penalty Band D where:
    (a) the damage is extensive or the value destroyed is substantial;
    (b) the damaged property is of particular cultural, historical, religious, or public importance;
    (c) the damage endangers life or safety;
    (d) the damage is committed by multiple persons acting in concert;
    (e) the damage is motivated by hate, prejudice, or intent to terrorize;
    (f) the damage is part of systematic vandalism or organized criminal activity; or
    (g) the offender has previous convictions for property damage.

5. Arson is the intentional or reckless setting fire to property belonging to another, or setting fire to one's own property in circumstances that endanger others or create a public hazard.

6. Arson is punished under Penalty Band E.

7. The penalty for arson is aggravated where:
    (a) the arson endangers human life;
    (b) the arson causes death or serious injury;
    (c) the arson destroys dwellings or buildings where persons are present;
    (d) the arson destroys property of substantial value or public importance; or
    (e) the arson is part of organized criminal activity or terrorism.

8. It is a defence to criminal damage if the accused honestly believed they had lawful authority or the owner's consent to damage the property, or if the damage was necessary to prevent immediate danger to persons or property and was reasonable in the circumstances.

9. The court may order restitution and compensation for the value of damaged property.
