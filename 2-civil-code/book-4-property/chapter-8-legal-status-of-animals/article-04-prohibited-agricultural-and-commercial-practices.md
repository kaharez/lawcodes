# Article 4 â€“ Prohibited Agricultural and Commercial Practices

1. The following agricultural and commercial practices involving animals are prohibited as inconsistent with the recognition of animal sentience and the duty to prevent unnecessary suffering:

## Battery Cages and Intensive Confinement

2. Battery cages and similar intensive confinement systems that prevent animals from engaging in natural behaviours and cause unnecessary suffering are prohibited for all animals, including but not limited to:
    a. battery cages for poultry (hens, chickens, quail, and other birds);
    b. gestation crates and farrowing crates for pigs;
    c. veal crates and individual calf confinement systems;
    d. any confinement system that:
    i. prevents the animal from standing, turning around, lying down, or stretching limbs fully;
    ii. prevents the animal from engaging in natural behaviours essential to well-being;
    iii. causes physical injury, deformity, or chronic stress;
    iv. provides inadequate space as determined by species-specific welfare standards; or
    v. is otherwise inconsistent with animal welfare principles.

3. All agricultural animals must be provided with:
    a. adequate space to move freely and engage in natural behaviours;
    b. appropriate environmental enrichment;
    c. access to outdoor areas or natural light where appropriate to the species;
    d. social contact with other animals of the same species where appropriate; and
    e. conditions that respect the animal's physiological and behavioural needs.

## Prohibited Mutilations and Alterations

4. The following procedures on agricultural animals are prohibited except where performed by a qualified veterinarian for documented therapeutic medical necessity:
    a. beak trimming, beak cutting, or debeaking of poultry for any purpose, including prevention of pecking behaviour that results from inadequate housing or management;
    b. tail docking of pigs, cattle, sheep, or any other animals, except where medically necessary to treat injury or disease;
    c. tooth clipping or grinding in piglets, except where medically necessary;
    d. castration without appropriate anaesthesia and analgesia;
    e. dehorning or disbudding without appropriate anaesthesia and analgesia;
    f. any cosmetic or convenience procedure that causes pain or suffering; or
    g. any other mutilation designated as prohibited by Sovereign Decree.

5. Where welfare concerns arise from management practices that would otherwise require mutilation (such as feather pecking), the management practices must be modified to address the underlying cause rather than resorting to mutilation.

## Chick Culling and Live Maceration

6. The following practices involving newly hatched birds are prohibited:
    a. killing of day-old male chicks by maceration, gassing, suffocation, or any other method (chick culling);
    b. killing of newly hatched birds due to sex, breed, or commercial considerations;
    c. live maceration or grinding of any birds;
    d. any method of killing young birds that is not immediately humane and painless; or
    e. disposal of live birds as waste.

7. The egg and poultry industries must:
    a. implement in-ovo sexing technology or other methods to determine sex before hatching;
    b. develop dual-purpose breeds that are commercially viable for both egg and meat production;
    c. ensure humane euthanasia methods are used where killing is unavoidable; and
    d. comply with any transitional regulations and timelines established by Sovereign Decree.

## Foie Gras Production

8. The production of foie gras by force-feeding (gavage) of ducks, geese, or any other birds is prohibited.

9. The following related activities are prohibited:
    a. force-feeding animals for the purpose of fattening their livers or any other organs;
    b. keeping animals in conditions specifically designed for foie gras production;
    c. importing foie gras or foie gras products produced by force-feeding;
    d. selling, offering for sale, or distributing foie gras or foie gras products produced by force-feeding;
    e. advertising or promoting foie gras produced by force-feeding; or
    f. serving foie gras produced by force-feeding in restaurants or food establishments.

10. Products containing foie gras must be labelled to indicate:
    a. whether the product was produced by force-feeding; and
    b. the country of origin and production method.

## Ritual Slaughter

11. The slaughter of animals without prior stunning (ritual slaughter) is prohibited except where specifically authorised by special licence granted by The Sovereign.

12. Applications for special licences for ritual slaughter must:
    a. be submitted to the Sovereign through the competent ministry or department;
    b. demonstrate legitimate religious necessity recognised by established religious authorities;
    c. provide detailed protocols for the slaughter method, including measures to minimise suffering;
    d. demonstrate that the applicant has expertise in the proposed method and animal welfare;
    e. specify the species, numbers, frequency, and locations where ritual slaughter will be performed;
    f. propose oversight and monitoring mechanisms; and
    g. address any concerns regarding animal welfare, public health, and humane treatment.

13. The Sovereign may grant a special licence subject to conditions, including:
    a. limitations on species, numbers, or frequency;
    b. requirements for veterinary oversight and monitoring;
    c. requirements for immediate post-cut stunning or other welfare measures;
    d. training and competency requirements for persons performing slaughter;
    e. reporting and inspection requirements;
    f. any other conditions necessary to protect animal welfare; and
    g. periodic review and renewal requirements.

14. Special licences for ritual slaughter:
    a. are granted at the sole discretion of the Sovereign and may be revoked at any time;
    b. do not create any right or entitlement to perform ritual slaughter;
    c. are personal to the licensee and may not be transferred;
    d. must be reviewed at intervals not exceeding five years; and
    e. may be subject to suspension or revocation for non-compliance with conditions or animal welfare concerns.

15. All other animal slaughter must be performed:
    a. with prior stunning rendering the animal insensible to pain;
    b. by persons trained and competent in humane slaughter methods;
    c. under veterinary oversight where required by regulation;
    d. in licensed and inspected facilities meeting welfare standards; and
    e. in accordance with best practices for humane slaughter as established by regulation and international standards.

## Cosmetic Testing on Animals

16. The use of animals for cosmetic testing, including testing of cosmetic products, ingredients, or formulations, is prohibited.

17. The following activities related to cosmetic testing are prohibited:
    a. conducting cosmetic tests on animals within Kaharagian jurisdiction;
    b. commissioning, funding, or contracting cosmetic testing on animals anywhere in the world;
    c. importing cosmetic products or ingredients that have been tested on animals after the effective date of this prohibition;
    d. selling or offering for sale cosmetics that have been tested on animals after the effective date;
    e. marketing or advertising cosmetics as safe or effective based on animal testing conducted after the effective date; or
    f. any other activity that directly or indirectly supports cosmetic testing on animals.

18. For the purposes of this Article:
    a. "cosmetic testing" includes any test, trial, or experiment on animals for the purpose of assessing safety, efficacy, toxicity, or other properties of cosmetic products or ingredients;
    b. "cosmetics" includes products intended for application to the human body for cleansing, beautifying, promoting attractiveness, or altering appearance, including skin care, hair care, makeup, fragrances, and personal care products;
    c. testing required for medical, pharmaceutical, or therapeutic products is not cosmetic testing;
    d. the prohibition applies to finished products and individual ingredients; and
    e. alternative non-animal testing methods, including in vitro, computational, and human-based approaches, must be used.

19. Cosmetic products must be labelled to indicate:
    a. that they have not been tested on animals;
    b. the date after which no animal testing was conducted; and
    c. any other information required by regulation.

## General Provisions

20. Transitional periods for compliance with prohibitions in this Article are established as follows, commencing from the date of entry into force of this Code:
    a. Battery cages and intensive confinement systems (paragraphs 2-3): 24 months;
    b. Prohibited mutilations and alterations (paragraphs 4-5): 12 months for new procedures, 24 months for phase-out of existing practices;
    c. Chick culling and live maceration (paragraphs 6-7): 36 months;
    d. Foie gras production (paragraphs 8-9): 18 months;
    e. Cosmetic testing on animals (paragraphs 10-13): Immediate for new testing, 12 months for sale of products tested on animals after entry into force;
    f. Religious slaughter practices (paragraphs 14-17): Subject to ongoing authorization for communities demonstrating historical dependence, with annual welfare review;
    g. Cosmetic product labelling (paragraphs 18-19): 6 months.

21. The Sovereign may, by Sovereign Decree:
    a. extend or shorten transitional periods upon demonstrated need and welfare considerations;
    b. establish exceptions for legitimate scientific research subject to ethical oversight and the three Rs principles (replacement, reduction, refinement);
    c. designate additional prohibited practices with appropriate transitional periods;
    d. establish welfare standards for agricultural practices;
    e. create licensing and certification schemes for animal agriculture;
    f. establish import and trade restrictions on products from prohibited practices; and
    g. provide for any other measures necessary to implement these prohibitions and protect animal welfare.

22. Existing operations engaged in prohibited practices must:
    a. cease the prohibited practices within the transitional periods established in paragraph 20;
    b. register with competent authorities and submit transition plans within 3 months of entry into force;
    c. transition to approved alternative practices in accordance with their approved plans;
    d. cooperate with inspections and monitoring;
    e. comply with any assistance or support programmes established to facilitate transition; and
    f. face closure or prohibition if unable or unwilling to comply within the transitional period.

23. Violation of prohibitions in this Article:
    a. constitutes breach of the duty of care under Article 2 of this Chapter;
    b. may result in criminal liability under Book V, Chapter 6 of the Criminal Code;
    c. may result in closure of operations and confiscation of animals;
    d. may result in prohibition from engaging in animal agriculture or commerce; and
    e. is subject to any other penalties provided by law.
