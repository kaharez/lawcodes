# Article 6 â€“ Animal Breeding and Reproductive Management

## Policy and Purpose

1. The State promotes responsible animal ownership and breeding practices, including the spaying and neutering of companion animals, to:
    a. prevent animal overpopulation and abandonment;
    b. reduce euthanasia of unwanted animals;
    c. improve animal health and welfare;
    d. prevent uncontrolled breeding and genetic disorders;
    e. reduce behavioural problems associated with intact animals;
    f. support animal welfare and public health objectives; and
    g. ensure that breeding is conducted responsibly and ethically.

## Promotion of Spaying and Neutering

2. The spaying and neutering of companion animals (including dogs, cats, and other species designated by regulation) is strongly encouraged and promoted through:
    a. public education and awareness campaigns;
    b. subsidised or free spaying and neutering programmes;
    c. partnerships with veterinary clinics and animal welfare organisations;
    d. incentives for owners of spayed and neutered animals, including reduced registration fees;
    e. requirements for shelters and rescue organisations to spay or neuter animals before adoption;
    f. support programmes for low-income owners; and
    g. any other measures established by regulation or local authorities.

## Registration of Intact Animals

3. While spaying and neutering are not mandatory, owners of intact (not spayed or neutered) companion animals must register their animals as intact with the competent authorities.

4. Registration of intact animals must include:
    a. identification of the owner and contact information;
    b. identification of the animal, including species, breed, sex, age, and distinguishing features;
    c. microchip number or other permanent identification;
    d. reason for keeping the animal intact (breeding programme, show, medical contraindication, or personal choice);
    e. confirmation that the owner understands responsibilities related to intact animals;
    f. any other information required by regulation; and
    g. periodic renewal as required by regulation.

5. Registration of intact animals is subject to a registration fee, which may be set higher than fees for spayed or neutered animals to:
    a. reflect the additional regulatory oversight required;
    b. fund animal welfare and population control programmes;
    c. incentivise spaying and neutering; and
    d. support enforcement and education efforts.

6. Owners of intact animals have additional responsibilities, including:
    a. preventing unplanned breeding and escapes;
    b. securing animals during breeding cycles to prevent uncontrolled mating;
    c. ensuring any breeding is conducted responsibly and ethically;
    d. complying with breeding regulations and restrictions;
    e. being accountable for offspring and ensuring their welfare; and
    f. submitting to any inspections or verifications required by regulation.

## Exemptions from Spaying and Neutering Requirements

7. While spaying and neutering are voluntary, certain categories may be subject to exemptions or specific requirements, including:
    a. animals in registered breeding programmes conducted by responsible breeders;
    b. animals used for showing, competition, or working purposes where intact status is necessary;
    c. animals for which spaying or neutering is medically contraindicated;
    d. animals participating in approved conservation or breeding programmes;
    e. service animals or working animals where reproductive status is relevant to their function; or
    f. any other category established by regulation.

8. Exemptions do not relieve owners of the responsibility to:
    a. register intact animals;
    b. prevent unplanned breeding;
    c. ensure responsible management; and
    d. comply with all applicable regulations.

## Regulation of Animal Breeding

9. Persons engaged in animal breeding for commercial purposes or on a scale beyond casual ownership must:
    a. register as animal breeders with competent authorities;
    b. obtain appropriate licences and permits;
    c. comply with welfare standards for breeding animals and offspring;
    d. maintain records of breeding, births, sales, and transfers;
    e. ensure genetic health and avoid inbreeding and genetic disorders;
    f. provide proper veterinary care, housing, and socialisation;
    g. screen prospective buyers and ensure appropriate homes for animals;
    h. submit to regular inspections and audits;
    i. comply with any limitations on breeding frequency, age, or number of litters; and
    j. meet all standards established by Sovereign Decree or regulation.

10. Commercial breeding operations must:
    a. be licensed and inspected regularly;
    b. maintain facilities meeting animal welfare standards;
    c. employ or consult with qualified veterinarians;
    d. avoid cruel or harmful breeding practices, including:
    i. excessive breeding causing harm to parent animals;
    ii. breeding animals with genetic disorders or health problems;
    iii. breeding for extreme physical traits that cause suffering;
    iv. separating offspring from mothers prematurely; or
    v. keeping animals in inadequate conditions;
    e. ensure transparency and traceability of breeding operations;
    f. maintain genetic diversity and health within breeding lines;
    g. provide lifetime support and take-back policies for animals bred; and
    h. comply with all applicable welfare and consumer protection laws.

## Prohibition of Harmful Breeding Practices

11. The following breeding practices are prohibited:
    a. breeding animals with known genetic disorders, hereditary diseases, or health conditions that cause suffering;
    b. breeding for extreme conformational traits that compromise welfare, health, or quality of life (such as brachycephaly causing breathing difficulties, skin folds causing infections, or skeletal deformities);
    c. inbreeding or line breeding that increases risk of genetic disorders;
    d. breeding animals too young, too old, or too frequently, causing harm to the parent or offspring;
    e. breeding without adequate veterinary oversight and care;
    f. operating puppy mills, kitten mills, or other high-volume, low-welfare breeding operations;
    g. breeding wild or exotic animals for pet trade except under approved conservation programmes;
    h. breeding animals for fighting, cruel entertainment, or prohibited purposes; or
    i. any other breeding practice designated as prohibited by Sovereign Decree.

12. Breed standards and breeding practices must prioritise:
    a. animal health and welfare over aesthetic or commercial considerations;
    b. functional traits and soundness over extreme physical features;
    c. genetic health and diversity;
    d. temperament suitable for the animal's intended role;
    e. ability to breathe, move, and function normally without surgical intervention;
    f. natural reproduction and birth without routine surgical intervention; and
    g. longevity and quality of life.

## Regulation of Animal Sales and Transfers

13. The sale, transfer, or gifting of animals is subject to the following requirements:
    a. sellers must verify that buyers are capable of providing appropriate care;
    b. animals must be microchipped or permanently identified before sale;
    c. health records and breeding history must be provided;
    d. minimum age requirements must be met (animals must not be separated from mothers prematurely);
    e. animals must be in good health and socialised appropriately;
    f. contracts or agreements must include provisions for return of animals if owners cannot care for them;
    g. sales in public places, markets, or online platforms may be restricted or regulated;
    h. transparency regarding breeding conditions and parentage must be maintained; and
    i. all applicable consumer protection and animal welfare laws must be complied with.

14. The sale of animals through pet stores, markets, or commercial outlets is subject to:
    a. licensing and inspection of facilities;
    b. prohibition on selling animals sourced from puppy mills or substandard breeders;
    c. requirements to provide information on animal care and needs;
    d. restrictions on species that may be sold;
    e. welfare standards for housing and care of animals in retail settings;
    f. traceability and record-keeping requirements;
    g. prohibition on impulse sales (such as mandatory waiting periods); and
    h. any other requirements established by regulation.

## Shelters and Rescue Organisations

15. Animal shelters, rescue organisations, and sanctuaries must:
    a. spay or neuter all animals before adoption unless medically contraindicated;
    b. provide veterinary care, vaccinations, and microchipping;
    c. screen adopters to ensure appropriate homes;
    d. provide information and support to adopters;
    e. maintain records of intakes, outcomes, and animal welfare;
    f. prioritise animal welfare and no-kill practices where feasible;
    g. operate transparently and ethically;
    h. comply with licensing and inspection requirements; and
    i. adhere to all standards established by Sovereign Decree or regulation.

16. Public or municipal animal control facilities must:
    a. work to maximise adoptions and minimise euthanasia;
    b. partner with rescue organisations and foster networks;
    c. implement spay/neuter programmes;
    d. provide humane care and housing for animals in their custody;
    e. ensure euthanasia, when necessary, is conducted humanely by qualified professionals;
    f. maintain transparency and public reporting on animal outcomes;
    g. offer lost and found services and owner reunification;
    h. provide public education on responsible ownership; and
    i. comply with all applicable laws and standards.

## Enforcement and Support

17. Competent authorities shall:
    a. establish and maintain registries of intact animals;
    b. provide accessible spay/neuter services and subsidies;
    c. regulate and inspect breeding operations;
    d. enforce welfare standards and breeding regulations;
    e. provide education and outreach on responsible ownership;
    f. support animal welfare organisations and shelters;
    g. monitor animal populations and welfare outcomes;
    h. take action against illegal or harmful breeding; and
    i. implement any other measures established by Sovereign Decree.

18. Failure to register intact animals or comply with breeding regulations may result in:
    a. fines and penalties;
    b. mandatory spaying or neutering at owner's expense;
    c. confiscation of animals;
    d. prohibition from breeding or keeping animals;
    e. revocation of licences or permits; or
    f. any other sanction provided by law.

19. The Sovereign may, by Sovereign Decree:
    a. establish specific registration systems and requirements;
    b. set registration fees and incentive structures;
    c. designate species subject to registration;
    d. establish breeding standards and regulations;
    e. create licensing schemes for breeders;
    f. establish prohibited breeding practices;
    g. provide for transitional periods and support programmes;
    h. delegate authority to local governments or agencies; and
    i. provide for any other measures necessary to implement this Article.

20. Local authorities may, within their competence:
    a. implement and enforce registration systems;
    b. provide spay/neuter services and subsidies;
    c. establish additional welfare requirements;
    d. regulate or prohibit certain types of breeding within their jurisdiction;
    e. support animal welfare organisations; and
    f. take any other local measures consistent with this Article and applicable law.
